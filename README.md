# Transcriptomic Signature of Metastatic Potential Stratifies Prognosis in Lung Cancer

  <img src="./images/Graphical_abstract.png" alt="Graphical abstract" width="900">
</p>

Metastasis is a major contributor to mortality in lung cancer, yet its molecular basis establishing metastatic competence remains incompletely understood. Most prior transcriptomic studies have relied on retrospective comparisons between primary and metastatic tumors, which often reflect late-stage consequences rather than early determinants. We leveraged the MetMap500 resource, which provides prospective and quantitative measurements of metastatic potential across 484 human cancer cell lines, to identify transcriptomic features associated with metastatic behavior. Through differential expression and co-expression network analysis, we identified a 13-gene metastasis-associated signature in lung cancer. This signature showed significant prognostic associations with metastasis-free, relapse-free, and overall survival when evaluated as a continuous risk score. In the TCGA LUAD cohort, higher signature scores were associated with increased hazards per standard-deviation increment across 1-, 5-, and 10-year follow-up after adjustment for available clinical covariates, including disease stage. Harrell’s C-index analyses showed consistently competitive discrimination relative to 14 published metastasis-related gene sets, while size-matched 13-gene subset controls indicated that its performance was not attributable solely to gene-set size. Elevated metastasis signature scores were also associated with increased resistance to broad anticancer agents. In addition, signature-based computational compound prioritization recovered crizotinib, an established targeted therapy for metastatic NSCLC, as the highest-ranked compound across both approaches. This finding provides a positive control supporting the internal consistency of the computational framework.
